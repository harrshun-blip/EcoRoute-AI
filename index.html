SDG 11 — Sustainable Cities

Goal 11 focuses on making urban settlements inclusive, safe, resilient, and sustainable. By opting for shared transit (bus, metro) or active options (cycling, walking), you actively reduce road congestion and localized smog, contributing directly to:

  • Target 11.2: Providing access to safe, affordable, accessible and sustainable transport systems for all.
  • Target 11.6: Reducing the adverse environmental impact of cities (air quality & waste).
SDG 13 — Climate Action

Goal 13 outlines urgent actions needed to combat climate change. Your decisions directly reduce greenhouse gas emissions, mitigating global warming. EcoRoute AI drives change by:

  • Target 13.3: Improving awareness, education, and human capacity on climate change mitigation and impact reduction.
  • Quantifying and visualising exact carbon offsets, turning environmental choices into tangible achievements.

Internship Evaluation Panel — Viva Q&A

Below is a curated set of questions and detailed answers designed to prepare you for internship project reviews, viva voce exams, and presentations.

Q1: How does EcoRoute AI align with UN Sustainable Development Goal 11?
EcoRoute AI aligns with UN SDG 11 (Sustainable Cities and Communities) primarily through Target 11.2, which aims to provide access to safe, affordable, accessible, and sustainable transport systems. The system exposes the true carbon and cost footprint of 15 different travel modes side-by-side, motivating commuters to transition away from single-passenger private fossil fuel vehicles to public buses, electric rail (metro), carpools, or active transport (walking and bicycling). By driving this behavioral change, the system directly helps reduce traffic congestion and per-capita urban air pollution (Target 11.6).
Q2: What is the core Multi-Criteria Decision Making (MCDM) algorithm used in this project?
The recommendation engine uses a Weighted Sum Model (WSM) with Min-Max Normalization. For every travel mode, it computes estimated travel time, financial cost, and carbon emissions. Since these criteria use different units (minutes, Rupees, grams of CO₂), they are normalized to a standard 0 to 1 scale, where 1 represents the best (lowest value) and 0 represents the worst (highest value). The user's sliders define weights for each criterion. The system computes a composite weighted score for each mode and ranks them in descending order.
Q3: How is traffic congestion predicted and how does it affect route calculations?
Traffic congestion is simulated using a predictive regression heuristic that models peak rush hours (8-10 AM, 5-8 PM on weekdays), weekend traffic relief, and weather factors (rain increases congestion). Road-based modes (cars, buses, auto-rickshaws, and e-scooters) have their average speeds reduced by this congestion factor (ranging from 1.0x to 2.0x), which directly increases their estimated travel times. High-speed rail transit like the Metro and Local Trains, as well as active pedestrian modes, are calculated as congestion-free (constant speed).
Q4: What data sources were used to determine the carbon emission factors?
Carbon emission parameters are derived from internationally recognized reporting standards. Baseline vehicle emission factors are based on the **DEFRA 2023** (UK Department for Environment, Food & Rural Affairs) greenhouse gas reporting database. These factors are adjusted for the Indian context: for example, private petrol hatchbacks are set to 170g CO₂/km, while the Electric Vehicle (EV) factor of 50g CO₂/km accounts for indirect emissions associated with India's electrical power grid (NITI Aayog / MoEFCC guidelines, which reflect a fossil-heavy grid mix).
Q5: How does the local storage database operate in this version of the app?
Instead of a traditional server-side database (like PostgreSQL) or a local SQLite file requiring a Python runtime, this application uses the browser's native **LocalStorage** API. LocalStorage stores key-value pairs persistently in the user's browser, representing database tables (like users and commute history) as serialized JSON arrays. The `DatabaseManager` class reads, parses, and writes to these arrays, ensuring that commute history and profile weights persist across sessions without requiring any background database engine.
Q6: How does the rule-based Eco-Coach AI generate personalized advice?
The Eco-Coach uses NLP pattern matching to classify user queries into 9 distinct sustainability domains (such as carbon impact, financial savings, benefits of cycling, public transit, carpooling, health, and electric vehicles). For each domain, it generates a comprehensive, contextual response detailing specific data points. If a user has recently calculated a route, the coach extracts details of that commute (distance, recommended mode) to compute custom carbon offset metrics (e.g., equivalent smartphone charges) to make the advice actionable.
Q7: What is the significance of the tree equivalence calculation?
The tree equivalence makes the abstract metric of "grams of CO₂" tangible for everyday users. According to forestry and environmental research (e.g., European Environment Agency guidelines), a mature tree absorbs approximately 21 kg (21,000 grams) of carbon dioxide per year. By dividing the user's cumulative saved CO₂ (grams) by 21,000, the system computes the exact "annual work worth of a tree" the commuter has offset, promoting positive environmental reinforcement.
Q8: Why is a petrol car used as the baseline for savings calculations?
Private single-occupancy petrol vehicles represent the highest-emitting mode of common urban transportation (170g CO₂/km). Using this as the baseline represents the maximum potential carbon cost of the trip. The savings shown indicate the emissions avoided by choosing a more sustainable alternative (e.g., metro rail emitting only 20g/km) instead of making the same journey in a standard private vehicle, highlighting the environmental value of the shift.
Q9: What are the economic savings associated with sustainable commuting?
Sustainable transport options have a much lower per-kilometer cost. In typical Indian cities, operating a petrol car costs roughly ₹8.5/km (fuel + basic maintenance). Carpooling with 4 passengers cuts this to ₹2.13/km. Public bus fares average ₹1.5/km, and metro fares are roughly ₹3.0/km. Active modes like walking and cycling cost ₹0/km. Therefore, shifting a 20 km daily commute to the metro can save a commuter over ₹2,200 per week in transportation costs.
Q10: What are the future enhancements planned for this project?
Future planned features include: 1) Integration with real-time navigation APIs (like Google Maps or MapmyIndia) for live routing; 2) Integration with live public transit APIs for real-time bus and train arrivals; 3) A mobile application shell built using Cordova or Capacitor; 4) Gamification elements including community leaderboards and badges; and 5) Partnerships with corporate employers to allow verified green commutes to be redeemed for local rewards or carbon credits.
